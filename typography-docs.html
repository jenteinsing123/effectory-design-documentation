Typography

Engage uses Poppins across all surfaces. A six-level heading scale and a set of body styles cover every context — from page heroes to dense data tables — with a dedicated responsive step for smaller screens.

🔤 Typeface

Poppins by Indian Type Foundry — a geometric sans-serif with clean, circular letterforms that feel modern without being cold. Three weights are used: Regular for reading comfort, Medium for subtle emphasis, and SemiBold for all heading levels.

📐 Heading scale

Six levels — L1 to L6 — form the heading hierarchy. All levels use SemiBold. L6 is the only uppercase style and is reserved for labels and overlines, not narrative headings. Values shown are for the Large (desktop) scale.

✨ Best practices

Use L1 once per view

L1 is the loudest level in the scale. Using it more than once removes its ability to anchor the page and makes every section feel equally important.

Engage your people
Your surveys
Recent activity
Do
One page-level L1 title anchors the view. Subsequent sections step down to L3 and L5.
Your surveys
Recent activity
Team overview
Don't
Repeating L1 for every section removes hierarchy — nothing reads as the primary focus.

Step through the scale — don't skip levels

Large jumps between heading levels break the visual rhythm. Each step in the scale signals a meaningful shift in content depth — skipping steps leaves the reader without clear structure.

Response rate
By department
Engineering · 84 responses · 79%
Product · 61 responses · 88%
Do
L3 → L5 → body-12 creates a clear information hierarchy at three distinct visual weights.
Response rate
Engineering · 84 responses · 79% · Product · 61 responses · 88% · Marketing · 43 responses · 72%
Don't
Jumping from a large heading straight to tiny body text strands the reader — there's no intermediate level to scan.

Reserve L6 for labels and overlines

L6's uppercase, tracked treatment is designed for short identifiers — column headers, category labels, overlines above a stat. It becomes illegible and exhausting when used for sentence-length copy.

Do
L6 as a data overline or table column header — short, scannable, clearly a label.
The survey has been completed successfully. Thank you for your participation in this quarter's engagement cycle.
Don't
Never use L6 for sentence text — uppercase at reading length is illegible and feels like shouting.

Default to body-14 — go smaller only for metadata

body-14 is the design system baseline for a reason. Reaching for body-12 to save space is a common mistake that makes interfaces feel cramped and hard to read, particularly for accessibility.

↕️ Headlines and body copy

The gap between a headline and the body copy that follows it depends on the headline's point size. Larger headlines need more breathing room; smaller ones stay tighter so the label–content pairing reads as a unit.

✅ Usage

Each style has a primary role in the product. Mixing levels arbitrarily breaks visual hierarchy — when in doubt, stay at body-14 (default) and step up only when a clear heading is needed.

Headings
text-l1
Page hero titles, empty-state headlines, onboarding splash screens. Use at most once per view.
text-l2
Major section headings ("Your surveys", "Dashboard"), dialog and modal titles, onboarding step titles.
text-l3
Card headings, subsection titles, drawer headers, wizard step names, sidebar panel titles.
text-l4
Widget headings, collapsible section labels, data group titles, compact panel headers.
text-l5
Form field labels, inline headings within body content, navigation section titles, list group headers.
text-l6
Data table column headers, overline labels above a section title, badge and tag text, metadata category labels. Always uppercase — never use for sentence text.
Body
body-18
body-18-SB
Editorial intros, marketing copy, onboarding explanations that need extra breathing room. Use sparingly inside the product UI — prefer body-16 or body-14 there.
body-16
Product descriptions, longer instructions, tooltip body copy, empty-state explanatory text.
body-14
(default)
The base UI text style. All standard paragraphs, feedback answers, survey response content, dropdown options, and form helper text. When in doubt, use this.
body-14-SB
body-14-M
Emphasised body text — highlighted values alongside data, active navigation items, selected states. SemiBold for strong emphasis, Medium for softer differentiation within a list.
body-12
body-12-M
Secondary metadata — timestamps, response counts, character counters, error messages beneath form fields, captions below charts and visualisations.
